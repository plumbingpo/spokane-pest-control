Rodent-Proofing Your Home in Spokane, WA

Home / Blog / Rodent-Proofing Your Home in Spokane, WA

Mice and rats are among the most determined home invaders, and once they get inside, they multiply quickly and cause real damage. Rodents gnaw through wiring, contaminate food, spread disease, and nest in insulation and wall voids. The most effective long-term defense is not trapping or poison alone but exclusion: physically sealing your home so rodents cannot get in. This guide from Spokane Pest Control covers practical exclusion strategies to keep rodents out of your Spokane, WA home for good.

Understand How Rodents Get In

It takes surprisingly little space for a rodent to enter your home. A house mouse can squeeze through a gap the size of a dime, and a young rat can fit through an opening no bigger than a quarter. They are excellent climbers and gnawers, capable of scaling walls, traveling along utility lines, and chewing through soft materials. Common entry points include gaps around pipes and utility penetrations, damaged vents, gaps beneath garage doors, cracks in the foundation, and openings where the roofline meets the walls.

Because rodents exploit such small openings, a thorough inspection of your home's exterior is the foundation of effective exclusion.

Seal Gaps with the Right Materials

Not all sealants stop rodents. Mice and rats chew through expanding foam, caulk, and plastic with ease. Effective exclusion uses materials they cannot gnaw through:

  • Steel wool or copper mesh packed into gaps around pipes and small openings.
  • Galvanized hardware cloth over vents, weep holes, and larger openings.
  • Sheet metal or metal flashing along chewed edges and the base of doors.
  • Concrete or mortar for foundation cracks and gaps.
  • Door sweeps and weatherstripping rated to close gaps beneath exterior doors.

Combining a gnaw-proof material with a sealant gives the most durable result.

Eliminate Food, Water, and Shelter

Exclusion works best alongside removing what attracts rodents in the first place. Store pet food and pantry goods in sealed metal or heavy plastic containers. Keep trash in lidded bins and clean up spills promptly. Fix leaks and remove standing water, since rodents need a water source. Outside, clear away woodpiles, dense vegetation, and debris near the foundation that offer shelter and cover. Keep tree branches trimmed back from the roof, as rats in particular use overhanging limbs to reach the upper levels of a home.

Why Exclusion Beats Poison Alone

Many homeowners reach for rodenticides first, but poison alone rarely solves a rodent problem. New rodents simply move in through the same openings the previous ones used, and poisoned rodents often die inside walls, creating odor problems and attracting other pests. Rodenticides also pose a serious risk to pets and wildlife. Professional rodent control pairs targeted removal of the current population with comprehensive exclusion so the problem does not simply repeat itself.
